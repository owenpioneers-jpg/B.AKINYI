Why Kenya's Criminal Justice System Keeps Failing the People It's Meant to Protect

Beryl Akinyi
Criminal Justice Management · Community Safety Advocate · Nairobi, Kenya
January 2025
· 7 min read
Reading progress
Share
“A justice system that punishes people for being poor is not a justice system. It is a class system wearing a wig.”

Walk into any Kenyan court on a Monday morning and you will see them — rows of people in remand uniforms, brought in from holding facilities that were built for a fraction of their current population. Many have been there for months. Some for years. They have not been convicted of anything. They are waiting. And the thing that keeps them waiting, more than any legal complexity or court backlog, is money.

Kenya's criminal justice system is not broken by accident. It is broken by design — a design inherited from colonial administration that was built to control populations, not to deliver justice. And while the architecture has changed, many of its foundational assumptions have not. The most damaging of these is the assumption that justice is something you can afford, or can't.

The Bail Crisis Nobody Wants to Talk About

In 2021, Kenya's National Council on the Administration of Justice reported that over 30% of the prison population consisted of remand detainees people awaiting trial who had not yet been found guilty of any crime. A significant portion of these individuals were held not because they posed a flight risk or a danger to the public, but because they could not raise the bail amount set by the court.

Consider what that means in practice. A young man from Mathare is arrested on suspicion of theft. He appears before a magistrate. Bail is set at KSh 50,000 a figure that may seem modest in some quarters, but for a family surviving on casual labour, it is several months of income. The family cannot raise it. He goes to remand. He loses his job. His family loses their source of income. He sits in a facility built for 200 people, sharing it with 600 others, for six months before his case is even heard. And when it is heard, the charges may be dropped or he may be acquitted. But the damage to him, to his family, to his community cannot be undone.

📊
The Numbers Behind the Crisis

Kenya's prisons operate at over 200% capacity. Remand detainees make up roughly a third of that population. The average time spent on remand before trial in Kenya exceeds 180 days for many cases and can stretch to years for complex matters.

The System Is Not Neutral

One of the most persistent myths about criminal justice is that it operates blindly that the scales of justice are truly balanced and that outcomes depend entirely on facts and law. This is a comforting fiction. In reality, outcomes in the criminal justice system are deeply shaped by socioeconomic status, geography, access to legal representation, and the particular magistrate or judge assigned to your case.

A defendant with a lawyer enters the courtroom with a fundamentally different set of possibilities than one without. The ability to challenge an illegal arrest, to apply for bond variation, to file an appeal — these are not theoretical rights available to everyone. They are practical rights available to those with the resources to exercise them. For the majority of Kenyans who cannot afford a private lawyer and who live in areas underserved by legal aid, these rights exist on paper and nowhere else.

“The most dangerous place in the Kenyan justice system is not a courtroom it is the remand cell, where people lose months or years of their lives before a verdict is even reached.”

What Reform Actually Requires

Reform conversations in Kenya often focus on the courts on reducing backlog, digitising files, hiring more magistrates. These are necessary, but they are downstream interventions. They treat the symptoms rather than the disease. If we want to fix what is broken, we need to be honest about what is actually broken.

First, bail policy needs a fundamental overhaul. The principle that bail should be calibrated to ensure attendance at trial, not to punish or to provide security, is already embedded in Kenyan law. But the application of this principle is inconsistent and often punitive. Community service bonds, supervised release, and non-financial conditions of bail are underutilised tools that could keep people out of remand without compromising court attendance.

Second, legal aid must be treated as a right, not a charity. The Legal Aid Act of 2016 was a significant step, but its implementation has been fitful and under-resourced. Every person who cannot afford representation should be guaranteed access to a qualified legal aid lawyer not a paralegal volunteer, but a trained professional with the time and resources to do the work properly.

💡
What Works: Community-Based Justice Mechanisms

Nyumba Kumi initiatives, community dispute resolution panels, and diversion programmes for minor offences have shown measurable success in reducing case flow into the formal court system. They deserve more investment, not less.

The Community Cannot Be an Afterthought

Here is something I have learned from years of working in this space: every person who enters the criminal justice system comes from a community. And when the system fails them whether through prolonged remand, wrongful conviction, or a prison experience that deepens trauma rather than addressing it it is that community that absorbs the damage. Families destabilised. Children growing up without parents. Neighbourhoods that associate justice with fear and loss, not safety and fairness.

This is why community safety cannot be separated from criminal justice reform. They are the same project. You cannot build genuinely safe communities on a foundation of injustice. And you cannot reform a justice system without understanding and centering the communities it is meant to serve.

This Will Not Fix Itself

I am not naive about what reform requires. It requires political will. It requires budget allocations that will face competition from other pressing priorities. It requires institutions that are deeply resistant to change to change. None of this is easy. But the alternative — continuing to operate a system that criminalises poverty, that holds people in overcrowded cells before they have been found guilty of anything, that delivers outcomes shaped more by wealth than by evidence is not an option that any of us should be willing to accept.

Kenya has the legal framework, the civil society organisations, the community leaders, and the professionals within the system who want change. What is needed now is the courage to name the problem clearly, to refuse comfortable half-measures, and to build the coalitions that can push reform from conversation to action. That work is already happening. It needs to go faster. And it needs more of us.
